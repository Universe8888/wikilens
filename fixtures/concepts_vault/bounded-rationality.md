# Bounded Rationality

In 1955, Herbert Simon published a short paper that quietly upended the dominant model of human decision-making. Classical economics assumed that agents possess complete information, unlimited computational power, and well-defined preference orderings — and therefore always choose the globally optimal action. Simon argued this picture is simply false as a description of how people (and organisations) actually behave.

The constraints he identified became the three pillars of what he called *bounded rationality*:

## The Three Bounds

### 1. Information Limits
Agents never have access to all the information that would be required for a truly optimal choice. The future is uncertain, alternatives may be hidden, and search is costly. A chess grandmaster does not "see" all possible game trees; a CEO does not know every competitor's cost structure.

### 2. Computational Limits
Even when information exists, processing it exhaustively exceeds the cognitive budget of any real mind. Working memory is small, attention is selective, and the time window for most decisions is finite. The brain is not a maximising calculator — it is a fast, frugal, approximate inference engine.

### 3. Time Limits
Optimal solutions often require more deliberation time than the decision context allows. A general under fire, a surgeon mid-procedure, or a trader watching a price move cannot pause to run a global optimisation algorithm.

## The Consequence: Heuristics Are Rational

Simon's key move was to argue that, given these bounds, using heuristics and shortcuts is not a cognitive failure — it is *adaptive*. Rational behaviour, properly defined, must be evaluated relative to the environment and the available cognitive resources.

The organism "adapts" its decision procedure to fit the constraints of the situation. A simple rule that runs fast and reliably beats a complex procedure that cannot finish in time.

> "The capacity of the human mind for formulating and solving complex problems is very small compared with the size of the problems whose solution is required for objectively rational behaviour in the real world."
> — Herbert Simon, 1957

## Influence on Later Work

Simon's framework seeded several research programs:

- **Prospect Theory** (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979) — documented systematic deviations from classical rationality under bounded conditions.
- **Fast-and-frugal heuristics** (Gigerenzen & Todd, 1999) — showed that simple rules often outperform complex ones in realistic environments.
- **Ecological rationality** — the idea that a heuristic must be evaluated in the environment in which it evolved, not against an abstract ideal.

## Connection to Search Behaviour

The direct behavioural implication is that real decision-makers stop searching when they find an option that is "good enough" rather than continuing until they can confirm optimality. See [[good-enough-decisions]] for concrete examples of this stopping rule in action.
