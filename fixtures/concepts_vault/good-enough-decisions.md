# Good-Enough Decisions

When a hiring manager reviews a stack of two hundred resumes, she does not read all two hundred and then rank them from best to worst before making an offer. Instead, she forms a rough mental standard — "can write clearly, has done something similar before, no obvious red flags" — and stops reading the moment she finds a candidate who clears that bar.

This pattern shows up everywhere humans make sequential choices under time pressure.

## The Search-and-Stop Pattern

The logic works like this:

1. Set a minimum acceptable level for what you need.
2. Evaluate options one at a time in whatever order they appear.
3. Stop the moment an option meets every minimum criterion.
4. Choose that option without continuing the search.

The search terminates not because the decision-maker has found the *best* option, but because she has found a *workable* one. The remaining alternatives never get evaluated.

### Examples in Practice

**Apartment hunting**: A renter who needs to be within walking distance of work, under a certain rent ceiling, and with at least one bedroom signs the lease on the first flat that checks all three boxes — even if a superior flat is three viewings away.

**Mate selection**: Evolutionary psychologists have documented that people tend to form a "minimum mate value threshold" and commit when a candidate surpasses it, rather than dating every available person and ranking them globally.

**Job offers**: Candidates often accept the first offer that beats their reservation wage rather than waiting for the theoretically best possible package, because the search itself is costly.

## Why Not Always Optimize?

The alternative — exhaustive search and global ranking — is rarely feasible:

- The option space is often unknown or unbounded.
- Each additional evaluation carries a time and cognitive cost.
- The "best" option may not stay available while you keep searching.
- Uncertainty means the ranking is itself unreliable.

Under these conditions, stopping at "good enough" often produces better real-world outcomes than attempting to optimize.
