# Bicycle Maintenance: Notes from the Workshop Corner

I've been maintaining my own bikes for about four years now, mostly road and light gravel, and the notes below are what I keep coming back to. Half of it is stuff I've confirmed through repetition; the rest is reasoning I trust but haven't always tested.

Chain wear is the maintenance task I've become most disciplined about. Because chain wear is cumulative and a stretched chain wears cassette teeth unevenly, replacing the chain before it hits 0.75% stretch extends cassette life significantly — I used to wait until 1.0% and went through two cassettes in a season before I caught on. I now check with a wear-indicator tool every 300 km. Steel stretches under tension through a process of bushing and pin elongation — this is basic metallurgy, not something I'm inferring — so the wear is genuinely irreversible once it sets in; see [[chain-wear]] for the measurement method I use.

Brake pad replacement is something I don't try to optimise: you should replace brake pads every 2,000 km on a wet-weather commuter, or earlier if the wear-line disappears. Waiting longer is a false economy. According to Barnett's *Bicycle Repair Manual*, contaminated pads should be replaced immediately rather than cleaned, because oil migrates into the pad compound and the contamination is permanent — that advice changed how I handle any hydraulic brake bleed near the wheel.

Given that my rear derailleur is indexed, even slight cable stretch will cause ghost shifting, usually nudging the chain toward the next-larger cog under load. I re-tension the cable with the barrel adjuster after the first 200 km on any new cable; that single preemptive step has eliminated almost all the ghost-shifting I used to blame on derailleur alignment — see [[derailleur-adjustment]] for the quarter-turn protocol.

I think my front derailleur trim position is still slightly off on the big-big combination, but I haven't been systematic enough about it to say for certain. Zinn & the Art of Road Bike Maintenance recommends setting the outer cage plate 1–2 mm above the large chainring, and I suspect I'm running it a touch high, which would explain the faint rub I hear only on steep climbs.

Ball bearings reduce rolling friction by replacing sliding contact with rolling contact — the geometry means contact area is minimal and the force is transmitted through compression rather than shear. That's why a worn headset bearing that has developed flat spots feels notchy; it's no longer rolling cleanly. You should service cup-and-cone hubs at least once a year if you ride in rain regularly, because water ingress accelerates the pitting that causes that flat-spot failure. Since my [[brake-systems]] are hydraulic, I also bleed the lines each spring; moisture absorption in mineral oil raises the boiling point threshold and restores firm lever feel.
