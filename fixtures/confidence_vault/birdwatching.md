# Birdwatching: Field Notes and Passing Theories

I spent most of this morning at the reservoir edge with my binoculars, watching a loose cluster of yellow-rumped warblers work through the shrubby margin where the cattails give way to young willows. I wonder whether they choose these [[habitat-edges]] deliberately because the transition zone concentrates insects from two different microhabitats at once, making foraging more efficient than either habitat alone. The warblers arrived a full two weeks earlier than my notes from last March, and because that gap is so consistent across three species I've been tracking, this spring must have been significantly warmer along the migration corridor than usual.

I think the small flock was a staging group rather than residents settling in — the birds were restless, feeding hard but not singing, which to my eye signals a refueling stop before the next leg north. A lone individual I kept losing in the willows was noticeably brighter than the others; that bird is almost certainly a male in fresh breeding plumage. According to the *Sibley Guide to Birds*, yellow-rumped warblers are the most widespread warbler in North America during migration and among the last to depart in autumn, a detail that matches my own October records exactly.

I suspect that [[warbler-migration]] timing tracks insect emergence more tightly than temperature alone, which would mean a warm March without a matching insect bloom might still delay departure from wintering grounds. I also wonder whether the reservoir acts as a navigational anchor — whether birds following the river valley use this open water as a landmark to reorient before pushing farther. One grebe was hunting just offshore in a manner I can only describe as methodical: long dives, short surface recoveries, always drifting slightly east. I have no good explanation for the directional drift, but maybe it follows a current I cannot see from the bank. Birds navigate in part by sensing the Earth's magnetic field, an ability well established in migratory species across taxa. By the time I packed up, a second warbler wave was already trickling into the willows, and I still had not seen [[bird-song]] from a single one of them.
