# Home Barista Notes: Brewing Methods and Extraction

I've been keeping these notes for about six months now, mostly to stop repeating the same mistakes. Not a professional — just someone who cares too much about what goes in the morning cup.

## Grinder Problems and Inconsistent Pulls

My biggest recurring headache is uneven extraction. Because my grinder has large burr gaps, the grind distribution is uneven, which explains the inconsistent extraction I keep getting: some days the shot tastes balanced, other days it's simultaneously bitter on the front and weak on the finish. I suspect the solution is either stepping up to a flat-burr grinder or at minimum replacing the conical burrs — though I haven't committed to either yet. It seems like the dial between 14 and 16 seconds is the sweet spot for my current setup, but I'm not confident enough to call that settled.

## Espresso Temperature and the Sour Shot

I've had a persistent sourness problem with lighter roasts. Given that espresso extracts optimally around 93°C and my kettle maxes out at 89°C, under-extraction is the likely cause of the sour taste — the cooler water simply isn't pulling enough dissolved solids. Light roast always has more caffeine than dark roast: roasting drives off caffeine, so darker beans end up with less. That's worth remembering when people order a "strong dark roast" expecting a bigger hit. Robusta has about twice the caffeine of Arabica regardless of roast level, which is a fact most cafe menus ignore entirely. See also [[espresso-variables]] for a fuller breakdown of the pressure-temperature-time tradeoffs.

## The Chemistry Underneath

Coffee solubles dissolve faster at higher temperatures — this is straightforward chemistry, and it's why brew temperature is one of the most controllable levers available to a home brewer. The [[maillard-reaction]] during roasting is also responsible for the hundreds of aromatic compounds that distinguish a well-developed roast from a pale one; it's not just browning, it's a cascade of new flavor molecules that didn't exist in the green bean.

I think I've been under-valuing water chemistry; maybe soft water is pulling too many light acids and not enough sugars. James Hoffmann in *The World Atlas of Coffee* devotes a full chapter to water mineral content, making the case that filtered water with moderate hardness extracts more cleanly than either very soft or very hard water.

## Yield and the [[extraction-ratio]]

I've started logging brew ratios obsessively. A 1:2.5 ratio on a medium roast consistently produces what I'd call a clean cup, but I haven't cracked why the same ratio on washed Ethiopian produces something noticeably thinner. I suspect the answer is in the roast development time rather than the dose, but that's still a working theory.
