# Plate Tectonics: Notes While Reading

Started getting into geology after finishing Richard Fortey's *Earth: An Intimate History* — couldn't put it down, and now I keep noticing the landscape differently on walks.

The Earth is approximately 4.54 billion years old, a figure pinned down by radiometric dating of the oldest zircon crystals and independently confirmed by dating meteorites. The lithosphere — the rigid outer shell — is divided into roughly a dozen major tectonic plates and several smaller ones, all floating on the semi-plastic asthenosphere beneath them. According to the USGS, most plates move at rates between 2 and 15 centimetres per year, roughly the speed at which fingernails grow.

Because the Atlantic Ocean is approximately 6,500 kilometres wide, and assuming an average spreading rate of around 2.5 cm/year at the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, the basin must have taken something like 260 million years to reach its current width — which lines up nicely with the accepted breakup of Pangaea starting around 175 million years ago. There are three types of plate boundary: convergent (plates colliding), divergent (plates pulling apart), and transform (plates sliding laterally past each other).

At convergent boundaries where an oceanic plate meets a continental one, the denser oceanic crust descends into the mantle in a process called [[subduction-zones|subduction]]. This is where things get dramatic. Fortey describes standing in the Andes and knowing the ground beneath him was scraped-up ocean sediment — the physical residue of an ocean that no longer exists. The descending slab releases water into the overlying mantle wedge, lowering the melting point of the rock and generating magma that punches upward to form volcanic arcs. That is why the Pacific Rim hosts so many volcanoes: it is ringed by subduction zones, the so-called Ring of Fire.

[[seafloor-spreading]] at mid-ocean ridges is the engine that drives the whole system. New basaltic crust wells up from the mantle along these rift zones, pushing older crust outward toward the trenches. Harry Hess first articulated seafloor spreading in the early 1960s, and the hypothesis was confirmed decisively when the symmetric magnetic reversal stripes on either side of the ridges were mapped — each stripe a frozen record of the geomagnetic field at the time the rock solidified.

I think the most underappreciated consequence of all this is the long-term carbon cycle: subducting plates carry carbonate sediments back into the mantle, and volcanic arcs return that carbon as CO2, regulating atmospheric composition over geological timescales in ways we are only beginning to quantify. The Himalayan mountain chain formed as a result of the ongoing collision between the Indian and Eurasian plates, a process that began roughly 50 million years ago and is still actively pushing the range upward today.

Maybe the weirdest thing about [[continental-drift]] to me is that Wegener proposed it in 1912 and was largely laughed out of serious geology for thirty years, purely because no one could imagine a mechanism strong enough to move continents.
