# Guitar Tuning: Notes on Temperament and Intonation

I've been playing long enough that I can hear when a chord is off, but not always long enough to know exactly why. Tuning the guitar has turned into its own rabbit hole.

The physics is solid ground to start from. The octave frequency ratio is exactly 2:1 — an A at 880 Hz vibrates twice as fast as the A below it at 440 Hz. Standard concert pitch sets A4 at 440 Hz, and every other note on the instrument is derived from that anchor.

Because [[equal-temperament]] divides the octave into 12 equal semitones, every interval except the octave is slightly out of pure tune — which is why open chords can sound fuller than fretted ones at the same pitch. The open strings form coincidental harmonic relationships that equal temperament's compromises don't destroy the way fretting does.

A guitar never sounds perfectly in tune in every key simultaneously. This isn't a setup problem; it's a mathematical constraint baked into the tuning system. [[Intonation]] work at the saddle can get each string accurate at the octave, but the frets in between are still placed according to an approximation.

I think I'm more sensitive to thirds being out of tune than to fifths — a slightly flat major third seems to bother me in a way that an equally-tempered fifth doesn't, though I can't say whether that's trained or just personal tolerance. I suspect that years of playing in standard tuning have made my ear accept the compromise fifths as normal, while pure thirds in an occasional just-tuned chord always sound startlingly right by contrast.

According to Frank Ford's intonation guide on Frets.com, nut slot depth is one of the most overlooked causes of sharpness at the first few frets: too deep a slot effectively lengthens the vibrating string at low positions and throws the whole first position sharp even after the saddle is set correctly.

[[Open-tunings]] sidestep some of this by retuning the open strings to a chord, so the most-used voicings sit on open or lightly fretted strings where the harmonic relationships are cleanest. That's probably why slide playing in open E sounds so consonant — most of the notes that count are harmonics of the open-string fundamentals, not equal-tempered compromises.

I think my ear training has improved more from playing acoustic than electric, because the acoustic gives immediate feedback when two strings beat against each other. Maybe that means I should do tuning drills unplugged even when I'm preparing for an amplified set — I haven't tested this systematically, but the unforgiving resonance of the acoustic box makes mismatches harder to ignore.

Because intonation errors compound across the neck, a guitar that is barely sharp at the second fret will be noticeably sharp at the twelfth — this is why setting intonation by comparing the open string to the twelfth-fret harmonic versus the fretted note is the standard diagnostic.
