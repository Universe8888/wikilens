# Home Gardening: Notes from the Raised Beds

The east bed has been puzzling me all season. I think the clay subsoil underneath is still compacting the drainage even after I added the raised layer, which might explain why the roots look stunted when I pull a test plant. Drainage has always been the silent variable in this garden, and I suspect the problem goes deeper than the topsoil layer I can reach with a fork.

Companion planting always improves yields. I have watched it too many times to believe otherwise — basil next to tomatoes, nasturtiums along the border, marigolds at every corner. The beds that skip companions consistently underperform, and I am not interested in running a controlled experiment to disprove my own eyes.

The tomatoes in the north corner have been getting blossom end rot despite regular watering. Because that bed gets afternoon shade from the fence, the soil stays wetter longer, which creates anaerobic pockets that block calcium uptake; that calcium deficit is what causes the tissue collapse at the blossom end — and this tracks with what I noted in [[soil-ph]], where low pH further inhibits calcium mobility in wet soil.

According to Eliot Coleman in *The New Organic Grower*, a soil temperature below 50°F significantly slows the microbial activity that converts organic nitrogen into plant-available forms, and he recommends waiting until the soil genuinely warms before transplanting brassicas outdoors. I have started measuring soil temp instead of going by calendar date, and it has saved two batches of seedlings from a cold setback.

Legumes fix atmospheric nitrogen through a symbiosis with Rhizobium bacteria in their root nodules, converting N2 gas into ammonia that the plant and surrounding soil can use — this is established biochemistry, not something I am reasoning from observation. That is why I always follow my beans with a hungry feeder like sweet corn in the rotation.

I have been experimenting with deep mulching around the squash, and I think it is suppressing the powdery mildew by keeping spore splash from the soil surface to a minimum. Maybe there is also a moisture-regulation effect that keeps the leaf surfaces drier at night, though I have not confirmed that.

The RHS Gardening handbook recommends a soil pH of 6.5 to 7.0 for most vegetables, noting that outside that range, nutrient lockout becomes the primary limiting factor regardless of how much compost you add. My pH strips have been reading around 5.8 in the south bed, which would explain the yellowing between the veins on the brassica leaves — classic signs of manganese excess that opens up at lower pH levels. I will add lime before the next planting.

The [[companion-planting]] notes I made last autumn are already informing this year's layout. [[composting]] has been the single change that moved this garden from struggling to productive — I am fully convinced the biology in finished compost matters more than any bagged fertiliser I have tried.
