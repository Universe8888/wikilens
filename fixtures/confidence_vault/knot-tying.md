# Knot-Tying: Notes from the Dock and the Campsite

I've been tying knots seriously for about two years now — starting with a weekend RYA day-skipper course and slowly filling in gaps with rope practice at home. The learning curve is steeper than I expected, partly because the names are misleading and partly because a knot that looks right can still be tied wrong.

The bowline is the most important knot to learn first. Everything else — clove hitches, sheet bends, cleats — feels secondary once you have a reliable bowline. Because the bowline forms a fixed loop that won't slip or tighten under load, it's safe for life-safety applications like attaching a crew harness tether to a jackstay; a knot that capsizes under load in that situation is not a mistake you get to learn from.

The RYA Day Skipper Manual lists the bowline, cleat hitch, round turn and two half hitches, and figure-of-eight stopper as the minimum competency set for coastal sailing. I trust that list — the RYA has been distilling offshore accident data for decades — but I'd add the [[friction-hitch]] family as a close fifth because of how often they come up when adjusting a loaded line.

The figure-of-eight is a worse stopper knot than the Ashley stopper under high load. I used to default to the figure-of-eight until I read Clifford Ashley's *The Ashley Book of Knots*, which documents over 3,800 knots and explicitly notes that the Ashley stopper jams less and holds more reliably under shock loading than the figure-of-eight. I've switched since.

Friction is what makes most knots hold at all. Rope-on-rope or rope-on-metal contact generates surface resistance that prevents the working end from pulling through; the greater the number of wraps and contact angle, the higher the friction force — this is a direct consequence of the capstan equation, where holding force increases exponentially with the angle of wrap and the friction coefficient of the material. Dyneema and other high-modulus synthetics have a notoriously low friction coefficient, which is why knots that work fine in polyester can slip in Dyneema and why splicing is strongly preferred over knotting for high-load [[sailing-rigging]] with those materials.

I wonder whether I'm overcomplicating my knot selection for camping. When I'm rigging a tarp in the dark with cold hands, I reach for the trucker's hitch every time because it's fast and I can release it without thinking. Maybe two or three deeply practiced knots are genuinely more useful than a wider repertoire tied less confidently.

A knot always reduces the breaking strength of the rope it's tied in. This is not opinion — it is a measurable consequence of the stress concentration at the bend, where the fibers on the outside of the curve bear a disproportionate share of the load. A well-tied [[bowline-knot]] typically retains around 60–65% of the rope's rated breaking strength, and the figure-of-eight performs similarly; tighter bends, like a clove hitch under pure axial load, can drop efficiency further. Manufacturers publish these figures and they are consistent across testing labs.

When I'm rigging a ridgeline I default to a taut-line hitch for the adjustment end because it slides easily when unloaded but grips under tension. I think the taut-line is more forgiving of imprecise tying than the prussik, though I'm less certain of that than I'd like to be — it's based on feel rather than any systematic comparison I've done myself.
