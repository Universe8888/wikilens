# Meditation Practice Notes

I think the quality of a session has less to do with how long I sit than with the conditions of the hour before — coffee, screens, whether I rushed here. Something about that pre-sit window seems to set the attentional floor I'm working with.

Because my attention keeps fragmenting in the first five minutes, the scatter probably reflects my cortisol rhythm rather than poor [[breath-awareness]] technique; it nearly always settles by the eight-minute mark regardless of what I do, which suggests the issue is physiological timing, not effort.

It seems like the "observer" quality — that faint capacity to notice I've drifted — is actually more trainable than [[focused-attention]] itself, and that cultivating it is the more transferable skill.

Lazar et al. (2005) found cortical thickening in the prefrontal cortex and right anterior insula of long-term meditators, which at least establishes that consistent practice produces measurable structural changes.

The [[default-mode-network]] is reliably active during mind-wandering and self-referential thought — this is one of the most replicated findings in cognitive neuroscience.

I suspect the sessions where I feel the least accomplished are often the ones doing the most work, because tolerating restlessness without quitting seems to be the actual practice.

Jon Kabat-Zinn argues in *Full Catastrophe Living* that non-striving is itself the core disposition — the goal is to have no goal — which I find both clarifying and somewhat maddening.

Body scans do something different from breath-counting; I think they access a more interoceptive layer of attention, though I couldn't say precisely how or why.

Because I tend to catastrophize lapses in practice — three days off and I feel I've lost everything — the continuity probably matters less than I believe; Thich Nhat Hanh's framing in *The Miracle of Mindfulness* treats each breath as a fresh beginning rather than a point on a streak.

Maybe the resistance I feel before sitting is not laziness but a mild threat response — the mind pre-empting the loss of narrative momentum that stillness brings.

Informal practice during commutes genuinely seems to transfer something back to the cushion, though I can't isolate whether it's the repetition, the variety of context, or just the accumulation of intentional moments.
