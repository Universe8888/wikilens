# Exposure and the Triangle — Personal Notes

I've been shooting for about two years now and the [[exposure-triangle]] still catches me out. Aperture, shutter speed, ISO — I know the names, but mapping them to a scene in real time is a different thing entirely.

The one physics fact I keep coming back to: ISO measures the sensor's sensitivity to light, and doubling the ISO value doubles the effective sensitivity, which is why every stop up in ISO introduces proportionally more noise into the signal. That part is not opinion — it's how the sensor physics work.

Because a wider aperture lets in more light, a faster shutter speed can be used in bright conditions without underexposing, which is why sports photographers shoot wide open in a stadium. The logic flows directly from the physics. Similarly, because a narrow aperture starves the sensor, ISO has to compensate — and that trade-off shows up immediately on the [[histogram]] if you know what to look for.

Morning light is always better than afternoon light. I'll stand by that even though I can't fully prove it — the low angle, the cooler color temperature, the way shadows fall long and soft. Afternoon light often feels harsh and flat to me.

Bryan Peterson argues in *Understanding Exposure* that most photographers over-rely on Program mode and never learn to see exposure as a creative decision. I think he's right.

I suspect I rely too much on aperture priority when I should be taking manual control, especially in tricky backlit scenes where the camera meters for the wrong thing.

Ansel Adams writes in *The Negative* that visualization — pre-seeing the final print before pressing the shutter — requires understanding exactly how every zone of the scene will map to a tone. That discipline is what separates deliberate exposure from guessing.

[[Depth-of-field]] and exposure are linked in a way I didn't appreciate early on. When I shoot portraits at f/1.8 to blur the background, I'm also choosing to use a faster shutter or lower ISO — the exposure triangle forces that negotiation every time.

I think the hardest part is not the math but the habit: reading the scene before reaching for the dial, not after.

Maybe the reason I mess up flash exposures is that I haven't internalized the inverse square law — light falls off with the square of the distance, so moving a flash from one meter to two meters doesn't halve the exposure; it quarters it.
