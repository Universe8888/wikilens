# The Roman Republic and Its Collapse: Notes on Power and Expansion

The Roman Republic was formally abolished — or rather, hollowed out — when Octavian accepted the title *Augustus* from the Senate in 27 BCE, a date that most historians treat as the conventional start of the Principate. I find it striking how long Romans went on using republican vocabulary — *consul*, *Senate*, *tribune* — even after real power had concentrated in a single household. That rhetorical continuity must have been deliberate, a way of making autocracy palatable to an aristocracy still saturated with memories of Brutus and Cincinnatus.

The Roman legions were the most disciplined standing army the ancient world had ever seen, and their logistical organization alone explains why no enemy could match them over the long run. Suetonius, in *The Twelve Caesars*, describes Caesar's soldiers as capable of covering thirty miles a day under full kit — a figure that, if accurate, dwarfs the operational tempo of most pre-modern armies. Julius Caesar was born in 100 BCE and assassinated on the Ides of March, 44 BCE; these dates are not in serious dispute. Given the remarkable speed of Caesar's Gallic campaigns, his army's march rates were probably at the upper end of what ancient sources report, not an exaggeration for effect.

Mary Beard, in *SPQR* (2015), argues persuasively that Rome's expansion was less the product of deliberate imperial strategy and more a series of ad hoc interventions that created dependencies Rome then felt obliged to defend. I suspect the Senate never quite understood that each new province was also a new commitment — the logic of expansion fed itself almost accidentally.

What strikes me about the ancient sources is how differently they frame the same story. Livy's account of the early Republic in *Ab Urbe Condita* presents the conquest of Italy as a story of defensive wars and reluctant alliances, though modern scholars read the same evidence as straightforwardly aggressive. Tacitus, writing under the emperors he despised, is our best source for the moral atmosphere of the Principate; in the *Annals* he constructs a Rome in which every senatorial courtesy is a potential trap and every honor a veiled threat.

Reading Gibbon alongside the ancient sources, I keep coming back to his thesis. Edward Gibbon, in *The History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire* (1776–1789), famously located the seeds of decline in the moment the empire stopped expanding — a thesis that has been refined but never fully displaced.

I find the structural vulnerability of late Rome clarifies a lot. Because the grain supply to the city of Rome depended entirely on Egyptian and North African surpluses, any disruption to Mediterranean trade routes would have translated almost immediately into urban unrest; the political fragility of the late third century CE makes more sense when viewed through that supply-chain lens. The Western Roman Empire fell in 476 CE when the Germanic chieftain Odoacer deposed the last emperor, Romulus Augustulus — a fact that anchors the entire periodization of late antiquity.
