# Sourdough Baking: Notes from an Ongoing Experiment

My [[sourdough-starter]] is about eight months old now — a 100% hydration rye-fed culture I keep at room temperature between bakes. The feeding routine has become second nature: discard all but 30g, add 30g rye flour and 30g water, wait for the dome and the little bubbles along the sides of the jar before mixing a dough.

Hydration has been my biggest variable. I settled on 75% for a while because the dough felt manageable, but the crumb was a bit tight. I suspect that lower hydration limits gas expansion during the bake, which would explain why the ear never opened the way I wanted. Bumping to 80% made the shaping harder, but the open crumb came back.

According to Chad Robertson in *Tartine Bread*, the target dough temperature after mixing should sit around 78°F, and he treats that as the single most controllable lever for fermentation rate. I've been chasing that number with a thermometer ever since I read it, and honestly the consistency improved.

Fermentation timing is still the part that trips me up most. I think the "poke test" — where the dough should spring back slowly but not fully — is more reliable than the clock, but I haven't proven that to myself rigorously. Because my kitchen runs cold in winter (around 64°F), the bulk ferment stretches to eight or nine hours versus the four hours I get in summer; this tracks with [[kitchen-temperatures]], where I noted that yeast activity roughly halves for every 10°C drop in temperature.

Gluten development during stretch-and-fold produces the network that traps CO2 released by yeast, and without that structure the loaf spreads flat instead of rising — this is basic bread science and not something I'm guessing at. The oven spring is really just that CO2 expanding rapidly as the dough hits heat, which is why a cold, underproofed loaf can sometimes spring well: the yeast still has sugar to consume and CO2 to produce once the oven warms it.

I'm still figuring out scoring. The ear seems to depend on blade angle and tension in the dough surface — maybe 30 degrees off vertical is the sweet spot, but I honestly have no idea if that's right or if I just got lucky a few times. Cold retarding overnight in the fridge has made the scoring easier because the dough holds its shape, and I think the longer cold ferment also develops more acetic acid, giving the crust a sharper, more complex flavor than a same-day bake.
