# Woodworking Joints: Notes from the Shop

I've been cutting joints by hand for about three years now, and the more I practice the more I think joint selection matters more than technique — a mediocre [[dovetail-joint]] in the right place still outperforms a perfect biscuit in the wrong one.

The mortise and tenon is the strongest joint for furniture frames. I use it for chair legs, table aprons, and anything that takes racking stress; nothing else comes close for that application. The dovetail, by contrast, is the joint of choice for drawer fronts and carcase corners — because the dovetail's mechanical interlock resists tension perpendicular to the glue line, it's preferred for drawer fronts where the pull-force is exactly that direction. The geometry does structural work that glue alone never could.

[[Wood-movement]] is the thing that humbles every beginner, and I'm still working out its implications. Given that wood moves with humidity changes and the joint runs cross-grain, a rigid glue like hide glue may actually crack before the wood does; this is why traditional craftsmen often left mortise-and-tenon shoulders slightly proud rather than flush. I suspect that most joint failures I've seen in old furniture are not glue failure but wood failure adjacent to the joint line, where seasonal movement accumulates stress over decades.

Jim Tolpin, in *The New Traditional Woodworker*, argues that the hand-tool tradition built joints to the tolerances the wood itself demanded — not to thousandths of an inch, but to the feel of hand pressure on a well-fitted tenon. I find that framing useful: it shifts the goal from precision measurement to tactile feedback.

Tage Frid, in *Tage Frid Teaches Woodworking*, is more prescriptive about the dovetail ratio: 1:8 for softwoods, 1:6 for hardwoods. Because hardwoods resist shear more effectively, the steeper angle can be used without the tails becoming fragile; a 1:8 ratio in oak would produce tails wide enough to split under a mallet blow.

Wood is anisotropic — stronger along the grain than across it — and modern adhesives form bonds that under direct tension actually exceed the tensile strength of the surrounding wood fibers; it is the wood itself that fails first, not the glue line. This is why a well-fitted [[mortise-tenon]] doesn't need clamp pressure to survive: the mechanical fit holds alignment while the adhesive sets, and once cured the assembly is as strong as the wood allows.

I still have not settled on a reliable method for [[dovetail-joint]] layout at the bench without a sliding bevel. I think a dedicated marking gauge set to the ratio might be faster than resetting the bevel for every board, but I haven't run the experiment carefully enough to be sure.
