# Heat Transfer in Cooking

Cooking is fundamentally a process of heat transfer: moving thermal energy from a heat source into food to transform its texture, flavor, and safety. Understanding how heat moves helps explain why different cooking methods produce different results.

## Conduction

Conduction is the transfer of heat through direct contact between objects. When a steak sits on a hot cast-iron pan, heat flows from the metal into the meat surface by conduction. The rate of conduction depends on the thermal conductivity of both materials, the temperature difference, and the contact area.

Dense metals like cast iron have high thermal conductivity and retain heat well, making them effective for searing. Food itself is a poor conductor, which is why thick cuts of meat cook slowly from the outside in.

## Convection

Convection transfers heat through the movement of fluid (liquid or gas). In a convection oven, a fan circulates hot air, which continuously moves heat to the food surface more efficiently than still air. In a pot of simmering liquid, convection currents distribute heat through the liquid.

## Radiation

Radiant heat transfers energy through electromagnetic radiation without direct contact. Broiling in an oven, cooking over charcoal, and using a salamander all rely heavily on radiant heat. The surface of the food absorbs infrared radiation and heats up rapidly.

## The Pan and What Stays Behind

When cooking with conduction, the nature of the pan matters significantly. Different pan materials have different heat retention and distribution properties. After searing food at high heat, browned deposits form on the pan surface. These deposits, and the culinary technique for incorporating them into a sauce, are a distinct topic from heat transfer itself.
