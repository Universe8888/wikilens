# The Maillard Reaction

The Maillard reaction is a chemical reaction between amino acids and reducing sugars that gives browned foods their distinctive flavor. It is named after French chemist Louis-Camille Maillard, who first described it in 1912.

## The Chemistry

When food is heated above approximately 140–165°C (280–330°F), amino acids and reducing sugars begin to react. The reaction produces hundreds of different flavor compounds, including pyrazines, furans, aldehydes, and ketones. This complex mixture is responsible for the characteristic flavors of seared steak, roasted coffee, toasted bread, and browned onions.

## Conditions Required

The Maillard reaction requires:
- **Sufficient temperature** — It occurs slowly below 130°C and accelerates above 150°C. This is why boiling (100°C) does not brown food; it keeps the surface temperature too low.
- **Low moisture at the surface** — Water absorbs heat and limits surface temperature. Drying the surface of meat or vegetables before cooking, or using high-enough heat to drive off surface moisture quickly, promotes browning.
- **Amino acids and sugars** — Different proteins and different sugars (glucose, fructose, lactose) produce different flavors.

## Maillard vs. Caramelization

The Maillard reaction is often confused with caramelization, but they are distinct:
- **Maillard**: requires both amino acids and sugars; occurs at lower temperatures; produces savory and complex flavors
- **Caramelization**: involves only sugars; requires higher temperatures; produces sweet, buttery flavors

## Practical Implications

When searing meat or vegetables in a pan, the browned, flavorful crust forms via the Maillard reaction. The brown bits that stick to the pan after searing — and the technique for incorporating them into a sauce — are a key element of many classic cooking methods, though the specific process is not covered in this note.
