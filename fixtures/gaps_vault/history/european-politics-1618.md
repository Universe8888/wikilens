# European Politics in the Early Seventeenth Century

The early seventeenth century was a period of intense political competition in Europe, dominated by the power of the Habsburg dynasty and the efforts of other states to check it.

## The Habsburg Bloc

The Habsburg family controlled two major power centers: the Spanish branch (ruling Spain, the Spanish Netherlands, the Kingdom of Naples, and vast colonial territories) and the Austrian branch (providing the Holy Roman Emperors). This created a potential encirclement of France and a dominant position in Central Europe.

## France and the Balance of Power

France, though a Catholic kingdom, feared Habsburg encirclement more than Protestant heresy. French policy under Cardinals Richelieu and Mazarin was explicitly aimed at weakening Habsburg power by supporting Protestant powers in Germany and Sweden. This pragmatic, interest-based foreign policy — funding enemies of co-religionists — was a notable feature of seventeenth-century diplomacy.

## Sweden's Rise

Sweden under Gustavus Adolphus was a major military power that intervened in the Thirty Years War in 1630, initially to defend German Lutheranism but also to secure Swedish dominance over the Baltic Sea trade routes. The Swedish intervention dramatically changed the course of the war.

## The Dutch Republic

The Dutch Republic had been fighting for independence from Spain since 1568 (the Eighty Years War). The war in Germany intertwined with Dutch-Spanish negotiations, which were eventually resolved as part of the same 1648 settlement that ended the Thirty Years War. The exact terms under which Spain recognized Dutch independence, and the territorial arrangements finalized in 1648, are not detailed here.
