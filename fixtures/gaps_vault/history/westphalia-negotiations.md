# Peace of Westphalia Negotiations

The Peace of Westphalia was negotiated between 1643 and 1648 in the Westphalian cities of Osnabrück and Münster. It ended both the Thirty Years War in the Holy Roman Empire and the Eighty Years War between Spain and the Dutch Republic.

## The Congress

The Westphalia peace congress was one of the first large-scale multilateral diplomatic conferences in European history. Delegates from nearly every European power participated, including representatives of the Holy Roman Emperor, France, Sweden, Spain, the Dutch Republic, and the various German princes and cities.

The negotiations were extraordinarily complex, involving territorial claims, religious rights, constitutional changes to the Holy Roman Empire, and the recognition of new sovereign states. The process took over four years.

## Two Simultaneous Treaties

The final settlement consisted of two treaties signed simultaneously:
- The **Treaty of Osnabrück** (between the Holy Roman Empire and Sweden, with French mediation)
- The **Treaty of Münster** (between the Holy Roman Empire and France, and separately between Spain and the Dutch Republic)

Together these are referred to as the Peace of Westphalia.

## Significance

The Peace of Westphalia is often cited as a foundational moment in the development of the modern international order. The specific territorial and religious terms of the settlement — which territories changed hands, how religious rights were allocated, what constitutional changes were made to the Holy Roman Empire — defined the political map of Europe for generations, though those specific provisions are beyond the scope of this note.
