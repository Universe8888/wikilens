# Chlorophyll and Light Absorption

Chlorophyll is the primary photosynthetic pigment found in plants, algae, and cyanobacteria. It is responsible for capturing the light energy that drives the light reactions of photosynthesis.

## Types

**Chlorophyll a** is the main photosynthetic pigment. It absorbs light most strongly in the blue-violet (430 nm) and red (680 nm) ranges, and reflects green light, which is why photosynthetic organisms appear green.

**Chlorophyll b** is an accessory pigment that absorbs blue (450 nm) and orange-red (640 nm) light and transfers the energy to chlorophyll a. It broadens the range of light wavelengths that can drive photosynthesis.

## Structure

Chlorophyll molecules have a porphyrin ring with a central magnesium ion and a long hydrophobic phytol tail. The porphyrin ring is the light-absorbing part, while the phytol tail anchors the molecule in the thylakoid membrane.

## Role in Photosystems

Chlorophyll molecules are organized into **photosystems** (Photosystem I and Photosystem II). Most chlorophyll molecules serve as antenna pigments that funnel absorbed energy to special reaction center molecules. The reaction center chlorophyll a molecules then initiate the electron transfer reactions that generate ATP and NADPH.

## Carbon Fixation

The NADPH and ATP produced using the energy captured by chlorophyll are used in a separate series of reactions that take place in the stroma of the chloroplast. These stroma reactions are responsible for reducing carbon dioxide into organic molecules, but they are distinct from chlorophyll's direct role in light absorption.
