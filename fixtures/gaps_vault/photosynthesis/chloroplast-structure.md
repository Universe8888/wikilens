# Chloroplast Structure

Chloroplasts are membrane-bound organelles found in the cells of plants and algae. They are the sites of photosynthesis, converting light energy into chemical energy.

## Membranes

Chloroplasts are surrounded by two membranes: an outer membrane that is permeable to small molecules and an inner membrane that is more selective. Between them is the **intermembrane space**.

Inside the inner membrane is the **stroma**, a fluid-filled space containing enzymes, DNA, ribosomes, and starch grains. The stroma is where certain key photosynthetic reactions occur — specifically the reactions that use the ATP and NADPH produced by the light reactions.

## Thylakoids

Inside the stroma is an elaborate internal membrane system called the **thylakoid**. Thylakoids are flattened, sac-like structures organized into stacks called **grana** (singular: granum). Individual thylakoids within a granum are called **granal thylakoids**. Thylakoids that connect different grana are called **stromal lamellae** or stroma thylakoids.

The thylakoid membrane contains the light-harvesting complexes, photosystems, and ATP synthase complexes that drive the light reactions. The interior space of the thylakoid is called the **lumen**.

## Division of Labor

Chloroplasts have a clear spatial division of function:
- **Thylakoid membranes**: site of the light reactions (ATP and NADPH production, oxygen release)
- **Stroma**: site of the reactions that use ATP and NADPH to build organic molecules from CO₂

The exact pathway by which CO₂ is converted into sugar in the stroma involves a cyclic series of enzyme-catalyzed reactions. This cycle is the central process of carbon fixation in plants and was worked out by Calvin, Benson, and Bassham in the 1950s, but its detailed mechanism is not described here.
