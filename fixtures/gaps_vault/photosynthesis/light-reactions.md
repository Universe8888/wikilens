# Light Reactions of Photosynthesis

The light reactions are the first stage of photosynthesis and occur in the thylakoid membranes of the chloroplast. They capture light energy and convert it into chemical energy in the form of ATP and NADPH.

## The Process

When chlorophyll and other pigments absorb light, they excite electrons to higher energy states. These high-energy electrons pass through the electron transport chain, which pumps protons across the thylakoid membrane to generate a proton gradient.

The proton gradient drives ATP synthase to produce **ATP**. Meanwhile, water molecules are split through photolysis, releasing oxygen as a byproduct and supplying electrons to replace those lost from the reaction center. The electrons ultimately reduce NADP+ to **NADPH**.

## Products

The light reactions produce:
- **ATP** (adenosine triphosphate) — the cell's primary energy currency
- **NADPH** (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate) — a high-energy electron carrier
- **O₂** — released as a byproduct of water splitting

These products are then passed on to the second stage of photosynthesis. The ATP and NADPH are essential inputs for the carbon fixation reactions, but the light reactions themselves do not directly fix carbon dioxide.

## Location

The light reactions occur exclusively in the thylakoid membranes. The electron transport chains are embedded in these membranes, and the ATP synthase complexes span the membrane using the proton gradient to drive ATP synthesis.
