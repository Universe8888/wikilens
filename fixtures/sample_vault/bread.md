# Bread

Bread is perhaps the oldest prepared food — archaeologists have found
flatbreads from 14,000 years ago. The basics haven't changed: flour,
water, salt, and time. Everything else is variation.

## What's Actually Happening

When flour meets water, two proteins — glutenin and gliadin — link up
to form gluten, an elastic network. Kneading aligns these strands. If
you use yeast or wild cultures, [[fermentation]] produces CO2 that gets
trapped by the gluten network, causing the dough to rise.

Baking sets the structure: heat denatures proteins, gelatinizes starches,
and evaporates water into the gas pockets, locking in the crumb. The
crust browns via the Maillard reaction — amino acids and sugars forming
hundreds of flavor compounds.

## Sourdough vs Commercial Yeast

Commercial yeast (*Saccharomyces cerevisiae*) is a single strain,
predictable and fast. Sourdough starter is a mixed culture — wild yeasts
plus lactic acid bacteria — slower but more flavorful, with better keeping
qualities thanks to the acids.

## The Stages

1. **Mix**: hydrate the flour.
2. **Bulk fermentation**: the first long rise. Strength develops.
3. **Shape**: form the loaf.
4. **Final proof**: the last rise, usually shorter.
5. **Bake**: hot and steamy at first (oven spring), then drier (crust set).

Most novice failures come from rushing the bulk ferment or overworking
the dough. Time is the best ingredient.
