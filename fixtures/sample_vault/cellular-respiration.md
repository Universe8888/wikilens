# Cellular Respiration

Cellular respiration is the set of metabolic reactions that cells use to convert
biochemical energy from nutrients into ATP, the energy currency of the cell.
It's often framed as the reverse of [[photosynthesis]]: glucose plus oxygen
yields carbon dioxide, water, and usable energy.

## Three Main Stages

1. **Glycolysis** happens in the cytoplasm and breaks one glucose molecule into
   two pyruvate molecules, yielding a small amount of ATP and NADH.
2. **Krebs Cycle** (citric acid cycle) happens in the mitochondrial matrix.
   Pyruvate is further broken down, producing NADH, FADH2, and a little ATP.
3. **Oxidative Phosphorylation** uses the electron transport chain in the
   inner mitochondrial membrane to generate most of the cell's ATP.

## Efficiency

A single glucose molecule yields roughly 30-32 ATP molecules through aerobic
respiration. Anaerobic pathways (like fermentation) produce far less — only 2
ATP per glucose — but run without oxygen.

## Where It Happens

Most stages occur in [[mitochondria]]. Glycolysis is the exception, running in
the cytoplasm. For more on the energy economy of cells, see [[atp-cycle]].
