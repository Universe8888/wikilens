# Emulsions

An emulsion is a stable mixture of two liquids that don't normally mix —
typically oil and water. Mayonnaise, vinaigrette, hollandaise, and
butter are all emulsions. So is milk, for that matter.

## How They Work

Oil and water don't mix because water molecules hydrogen-bond with each
other and exclude the oil. An emulsifier — a molecule with one end that
likes water and one end that likes oil — bridges the two. Egg yolk
(lecithin) is the classic kitchen emulsifier. Mustard works too.
Without an emulsifier, any shaking-up is temporary; with one, droplets
stay dispersed.

## Mayonnaise

Whisk egg yolk with a little acid (lemon or vinegar) and salt. Slowly —
drop by drop at first, then in a thin stream — drizzle in neutral oil
while whisking. You're creating microscopic oil droplets coated in
lecithin and suspended in the water phase.

Classic failures: adding oil too fast (emulsion breaks, yolk can't coat
enough droplets); using cold ingredients (interface doesn't form
smoothly); over-beating (droplets coalesce).

## Saving a Broken Emulsion

Start a fresh yolk in a clean bowl. Slowly drizzle the broken mixture
into the new yolk while whisking. Often it re-emulsifies cleanly.

## Wider Family

Vinaigrette is a temporary emulsion — stable for minutes unless you add
an emulsifier like mustard or honey. Hollandaise is an oil-in-water
emulsion stabilized by egg yolk plus heat. Butter is a water-in-oil
emulsion, flipped from the usual direction. See [[water]] for why this
is all so stubborn.
