# Epistemology

Epistemology is the branch of philosophy concerned with knowledge: what
it is, how we get it, and what its limits are. "How do you know?" is the
perennial epistemological question.

## Classical Definition

Plato offered knowledge as "justified true belief." You know something
when (1) you believe it, (2) it is true, and (3) you have good reason to
believe it. Twentieth-century philosopher Edmund Gettier poked holes in
this definition with counterexamples — situations where all three
conditions hold but we'd hesitate to call it knowledge. Fixing "the
Gettier problem" remains an active area of debate.

## Sources of Knowledge

- **Perception**: what our senses tell us. Often reliable, occasionally
  deceptive.
- **Reason**: what we can derive through logic and inference.
- **Testimony**: what we learn from others. Vast, but only as reliable
  as its sources.
- **Memory**: our access to past knowledge, itself fallible.
- **Introspection**: direct access to our own mental states.

## Skepticism

Skeptics challenge whether any of these sources can deliver genuine
knowledge. Descartes' dream argument, Hume's problem of induction, and
Putnam's brain-in-a-vat scenario all test how much certainty we can
really claim.

## Connection to Science

The [[scientific-method]] is, in a sense, applied epistemology: a set of
disciplined practices for generating reliable knowledge about the world.
