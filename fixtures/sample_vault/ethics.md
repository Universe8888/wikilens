# Ethics

Ethics — sometimes called moral philosophy — investigates questions of
right and wrong conduct. What makes an action good? What do we owe
others? How should we live?

## Three Main Traditions

**Consequentialism** judges actions by their outcomes. Utilitarianism,
the best-known consequentialist theory, holds that the right action is
the one producing the greatest overall well-being. Jeremy Bentham and
John Stuart Mill were foundational figures.

**Deontology** judges actions by rules or duties, independent of
outcomes. Immanuel Kant argued that lying is always wrong, even to save
a life, because it treats others as mere means. The categorical
imperative tests an action by asking whether you could will its
principle as a universal law.

**Virtue ethics** focuses on character rather than actions or rules.
Aristotle asked: what traits make a flourishing human life? Courage,
honesty, temperance, justice, practical wisdom — the virtues — are
cultivated through habit. [[Stoicism]] is a close cousin.

## Applied Ethics

Modern work often applies these frameworks to concrete problems:
bioethics, environmental ethics, business ethics, AI ethics. These
fields ask how classical theories hold up in domains the ancients
couldn't have imagined.

## Meta-Ethics

A further level asks about the nature of ethical claims themselves.
Are moral statements objectively true? Merely expressions of
preference? Social constructs? Different answers lead to very
different ethical worlds.
