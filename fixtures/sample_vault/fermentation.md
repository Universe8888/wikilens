# Fermentation

Fermentation is controlled microbial transformation of food. Humans have
been doing it for at least 7,000 years — for preservation, for flavor,
and (not coincidentally) for alcohol.

## What's Happening Biochemically

Microbes — typically yeasts or lactic acid bacteria — consume sugars and
produce something else: CO2, alcohol, lactic acid, acetic acid. This
overlaps with [[cellular-respiration]] but usually happens anaerobically,
without oxygen.

The chemistry changes the food profoundly: new flavors from hundreds of
metabolic byproducts, altered textures, and — critically — lowered pH or
elevated alcohol that inhibits spoilage organisms.

## Major Families

- **Lacto-fermentation**: vegetables in brine. Sauerkraut, kimchi,
  pickles (the real kind, not vinegar pickles). The salt selects for
  lactic acid bacteria; they acidify, which preserves.
- **Yeast fermentation**: beer, wine, [[bread]]. Yeast eats sugar,
  produces CO2 and ethanol.
- **Acetic fermentation**: vinegar. Alcohol + oxygen + acetobacter →
  acetic acid.
- **Mold fermentation**: soy sauce, miso, tempeh, blue cheese, salami.
  Specific molds (*Aspergillus*, *Penicillium*) transform proteins and
  starches.

## Safety

Vegetable lacto-fermentation is very safe — the rapid acidification
outcompetes pathogens. Meat and dairy fermentation are trickier;
temperature and hygiene matter. When in doubt, start with cabbage.

## Flavor Depth

A ferment is almost always more interesting than its unfermented
starting point. This is a generalization, but it's a reliable one.
