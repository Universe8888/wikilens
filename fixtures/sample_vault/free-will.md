# Free Will

The free will debate asks whether our choices are genuinely our own — or
whether they're the inevitable output of prior causes (genes, upbringing,
neurons firing).

## Three Main Positions

**Libertarian free will** (not the political kind): we have genuine
causal power over our actions, not fully determined by prior events. The
self is a real initiator of choices.

**Hard determinism**: every event, including every mental event, is the
product of prior causes. There's no room for an uncaused choice. Free
will is an illusion, though a useful one.

**Compatibilism**: free will and determinism are compatible. Being
"free" doesn't mean "uncaused" — it means acting on one's own desires,
absent external coercion. A coerced signature isn't free; an uncoerced
signature is, regardless of whether the brain states leading to it were
determined.

## Modern Challenges

Neuroscience has complicated the picture. Benjamin Libet's famous
experiments suggested that brain activity corresponding to a decision
precedes conscious awareness of the decision by several hundred
milliseconds. Interpretations vary wildly — some take this as evidence
that conscious will is post-hoc; others argue the experiments don't
really test what they seem to test.

## Why It Matters

Free will is tied to moral responsibility, punishment, praise, and how
we think about ourselves and others. [[Ethics]], [[mind-body-problem]],
and legal theory all depend on it implicitly. [[Stoicism]] took a
distinctive position: we may not control what happens, but we control
our responses — a form of freedom that sits oddly in the compatibilist
camp.
