# Gravity

Gravity is the attraction between any two masses. It is by far the weakest
of the four fundamental forces, but its long range and universally
attractive nature make it dominant on astronomical scales.

## Newton's View

Isaac Newton's law of universal gravitation (1687) states that every mass
attracts every other mass with a force proportional to the product of
their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance
between them:

`F = G * (m1 * m2) / r^2`

Newton's formulation was spectacularly successful for predicting planetary
motion, tides, and projectiles — but it treated gravity as an instantaneous
action at a distance, which bothered even Newton himself.

## Einstein's View

[[relativity]] reframed gravity entirely. Rather than a force, Einstein's
general relativity describes gravity as the curvature of spacetime by mass
and energy. Objects move along geodesics — the straightest possible paths
through curved spacetime. A falling apple isn't being pulled; it's simply
following the shortest path through the warped geometry near Earth.

## Still Mysterious

Despite centuries of work, gravity remains the least-understood fundamental
force. No successful quantum theory of gravity exists yet — reconciling
general relativity with quantum mechanics is one of the biggest open
problems in physics.
