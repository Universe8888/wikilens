# Industrial Revolution

The Industrial Revolution was the transition from agrarian, hand-tool
economies to machine-based, factory-based production. It began in
Britain around 1760 and spread over the next century to the rest of
Europe and North America. Arguably no event since the agricultural
revolution 10,000 years earlier has reshaped human life more drastically.

## Core Technologies

- **Steam engine**: Thomas Newcomen (1712) and James Watt (1769)
  progressively made steam power practical. Steam freed factories from
  being located near rivers and powered the railways that followed.
- **Textile machinery**: the spinning jenny, water frame, and power loom
  mechanized cloth production. Textiles were the industry that first
  scaled.
- **Iron and steel**: Bessemer's 1856 converter made cheap mass steel
  possible, enabling railways, bridges, ships, and skyscrapers.
- **Electricity and chemicals** followed in the "Second Industrial
  Revolution" from about 1870.

## Why Britain First

A combination of factors: coal reserves near population centers, a
relatively open political system, colonial markets, an earlier
agricultural revolution that freed up labor, a scientific culture,
and — critically — wage levels high enough to motivate labor-saving
invention. Historians still debate the exact weights.

## Consequences

GDP per capita, which had been roughly flat for millennia, started
rising and hasn't stopped. Life expectancy eventually followed
(after a century of urban squalor). Global inequality exploded as
industrializing nations pulled decisively ahead.

And, critically, fossil fuels entered the human story at scale. Every
conversation about climate change is, in a sense, a conversation
about the Industrial Revolution.
