# The Mind-Body Problem

How do subjective mental experiences — thoughts, feelings, the redness of
red — relate to physical processes in the brain? This question, called
the mind-body problem, has haunted philosophy since antiquity.

## Dualism

Descartes famously argued that mind and body are distinct substances.
The body is extended in space and obeys physical laws; the mind is
unextended thinking substance. He proposed the pineal gland as their
meeting point — which satisfied no one, including him.

Dualism has intuitive appeal: my experience of pain seems different in
kind from the neural events correlated with it. But it struggles to
explain how two utterly different substances could causally interact.

## Physicalism

Most contemporary philosophers are physicalists: mental states just are
physical states (brain states). Variants differ on how tight the
identification is — type identity, token identity, functionalism.

## The Hard Problem

David Chalmers distinguished "easy problems" of consciousness (explaining
attention, memory, behavior) from "the hard problem": why is there
subjective experience at all? Why isn't information processing
accompanied by nothing? Even a complete physical description of the
brain seems to leave something out.

## Connections

The mind-body problem threads through [[free-will]], [[ethics]], and
[[epistemology]]. If mental events are physical, they're presumably
subject to the same causation as anything else — which bears directly on
whether "choice" is a meaningful concept.
