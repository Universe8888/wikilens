# Mitochondria

Mitochondria are double-membrane-bound organelles found in most eukaryotic
cells. Often called "the powerhouse of the cell," they're the primary site of
ATP production via [[cellular-respiration]].

## Structure

The outer membrane is smooth and relatively permeable. The inner membrane is
heavily folded into structures called cristae, which increase surface area for
the electron transport chain. The space inside — the matrix — contains enzymes,
mitochondrial DNA, and ribosomes.

## Endosymbiotic Origin

A leading hypothesis holds that mitochondria evolved from free-living bacteria
that were engulfed by an ancestral eukaryotic cell billions of years ago and
then retained a symbiotic relationship. Evidence for this includes:

- Mitochondria have their own circular DNA, similar to bacteria.
- They have ribosomes closer in size to bacterial ribosomes than eukaryotic ones.
- They reproduce by binary fission, independently of the host cell's division.

## Beyond Energy

Mitochondria also play roles in calcium signaling, apoptosis (programmed cell
death), and heat production. Dysfunction has been linked to aging, Parkinson's
disease, and several metabolic disorders.
