# The Printing Press

Johannes Gutenberg's movable-type printing press, developed in Mainz
around 1440, didn't invent printing — block printing had existed in
East Asia for centuries. Gutenberg's innovation was the combination:
cast metal movable type, oil-based ink, a screw press adapted from
winemaking, and a production workflow that made books cheap.

## What Changed

In 1450, a book was a luxury good — copied by hand, months of labor,
prices that excluded everyone but the very rich. By 1500, there were an
estimated 20 million printed books in Europe. By 1600, perhaps 200
million. The cost collapse was staggering.

This changed who could read, what was read, how fast ideas spread, and
which ideas survived. The Protestant Reformation is hard to imagine
without the printing press — Luther's 95 Theses were reproduced and
distributed across Germany within weeks.

## The Scientific Revolution

Science is a cumulative enterprise: you stand on the shoulders of those
who came before. That stacking only works if their work can be reliably
accessed. The printing press turned science from a whisper network among
scholars into a written conversation across continents.

Copernicus, Galileo, Kepler, and Newton all published their work in
printed books that traveled farther and survived better than any
manuscript could have. The connection between print and the
[[scientific-method]] is direct.

## Caveats

Historians caution against "great invention" narratives. The press
needed literacy, paper, Latin as a scholarly lingua franca, and a
bourgeoisie that wanted to read. Remove any of those, and Gutenberg's
press sits in a museum.
