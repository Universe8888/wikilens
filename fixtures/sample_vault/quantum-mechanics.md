# Quantum Mechanics

Quantum mechanics is the physics of the very small — atoms, subatomic
particles, photons. It replaced classical mechanics at those scales in the
early 20th century, because the old picture simply failed to predict what
experiments saw.

## Core Ideas

- **Quantization**: energy, angular momentum, and certain other quantities
  come in discrete chunks, not continuous ranges. An electron in an
  [[atoms|atom]] can occupy only specific energy levels, not anything in
  between.
- **Superposition**: until measured, a particle can exist in a combination
  of multiple states at once. Schrödinger's cat is the famous metaphor.
- **Uncertainty principle**: you cannot simultaneously know both the
  position and momentum of a particle with arbitrary precision. The more
  precisely you pin one down, the fuzzier the other becomes.
- **Entanglement**: two particles can be correlated so strongly that
  measuring one instantly determines the state of the other, regardless
  of distance. Einstein called this "spooky action at a distance."

## Interpretations

The math of quantum mechanics is well-established and works astonishingly
well. What the math *means* is still debated:

- **Copenhagen**: the wave function collapses when observed.
- **Many-worlds**: every possibility plays out in a branching multiverse.
- **Pilot-wave**: particles have definite positions guided by a wave.

No experiment has yet decisively distinguished between these interpretations.

## Relation to Gravity

Quantum mechanics and [[relativity]] are both extraordinarily successful
within their domains but are famously incompatible. Unifying them is one
of physics' great open problems.
