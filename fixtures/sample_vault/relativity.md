# Relativity

Relativity is a pair of theories by Albert Einstein that reshaped our
understanding of space, time, and [[gravity]].

## Special Relativity (1905)

Based on two postulates:
1. The laws of physics are the same in all inertial reference frames.
2. The speed of light in vacuum is constant for all observers.

These innocuous-looking assumptions force some wild consequences:

- **Time dilation**: moving clocks run slow (from the stationary observer's
  perspective). GPS satellites correct for this daily.
- **Length contraction**: moving objects shorten along their direction of
  motion.
- **Mass-energy equivalence**: E = mc^2, perhaps the most famous equation
  in science.
- **No absolute simultaneity**: two events simultaneous in one frame may
  not be in another.

## General Relativity (1915)

Extends the principle to non-inertial frames and gravitating masses. Its
central insight: gravity is not a force but a feature of spacetime
geometry. Massive objects warp spacetime, and that warping is what we
experience as gravitational attraction.

Predictions confirmed over a century: light bending around the sun (1919
eclipse), time running slower in gravitational wells, black holes,
gravitational waves (first detected 2015), and the expansion of the
universe.

## Compatibility Problem

General relativity works at large scales. Quantum mechanics works at small
scales. The two are deeply incompatible — merging them into a quantum
theory of gravity is among the deepest unsolved problems in physics.
