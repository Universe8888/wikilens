# Roman Empire

The Roman Empire was the post-Republican phase of Rome, conventionally
dated from 27 BC (when Augustus became the first emperor) to 476 AD in
the West, or 1453 in the East (fall of Constantinople). At its height
around 117 AD, it spanned roughly 5 million square kilometers and
encompassed perhaps 70 million people — about a fifth of the world's
population at the time.

## From Republic to Empire

Rome was a republic for nearly 500 years before the transition. A series
of late-Republic civil wars — between Marius and Sulla, then Caesar and
Pompey, then Octavian and Antony — ended with Octavian (rebranded as
Augustus) holding all effective power while preserving the outward forms
of the Republic.

## Infrastructure and Administration

Roman innovations in infrastructure still shape the Mediterranean: roads
(some still in use), aqueducts, sewers, concrete, law codes. The empire
held together through a combination of military presence, road networks,
administrative efficiency, and selective citizenship — by the 3rd
century, any free man in the empire could be a Roman citizen.

## Decline

The Western empire's decline is over-explained. Gibbon blamed
Christianity. Others point to overextension, currency debasement,
plagues, climate, barbarian migrations, or internal dysfunction.
Probably all of these to varying degrees.

The Eastern empire — what we now call Byzantium — lasted another
thousand years after the West fell, and at times was the richest,
most sophisticated state in the world.
