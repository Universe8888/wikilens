# The Scientific Method

The "scientific method" is less a single procedure than a family of
practices for generating reliable knowledge about the world. Textbooks
often present a clean five-step recipe; real science is messier.

## The Textbook Version

1. Observe a phenomenon.
2. Formulate a hypothesis.
3. Design an experiment to test the hypothesis.
4. Collect and analyze data.
5. Refine the hypothesis based on results.

This captures something real, but glosses over how hypotheses actually
get chosen, how experiments actually get designed, and how much
judgment calls shape conclusions.

## What the Textbook Misses

- **Theory-ladenness**: observations aren't neutral. What you even
  notice depends on the theory you bring.
- **Replication**: a single experiment, however clean, proves little.
  The "replication crisis" in psychology and medicine (2010s–2020s)
  exposed how much published work doesn't hold up when re-run.
- **Peer review**: imperfect but essential social filtering.
- **Falsifiability** (Popper): a hypothesis must make predictions that
  could, in principle, prove it wrong. "It rains because the rain gods
  want it to" is unfalsifiable, hence unscientific.
- **Accumulation**: science is cumulative. The [[printing-press]] made
  that cumulation possible at scale.

## Connection to Epistemology

[[Epistemology]] asks what knowledge is; the scientific method is one
tradition's practical answer. Imperfect, self-correcting, embarrassingly
slow — and the best truth-seeking engine humanity has yet built.
