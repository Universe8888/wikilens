# The Silk Road

"The Silk Road" is a modern name (coined by geographer Ferdinand von
Richthofen in 1877) for the network of trade routes that connected China
with the Mediterranean from roughly 130 BC to the 15th century. It was
never a single road — more a shifting web of caravans, rivers, and
seaports.

## What Moved

Silk went west. So did spices, jade, porcelain, and lacquerware. Going
east: horses, gold, glass, woolens. Sometimes, devastatingly, plague —
the Black Death rode the Silk Road to Europe.

But goods were only part of the traffic. Ideas, religions (Buddhism,
Christianity, Islam), technologies (papermaking, gunpowder, the
compass), and languages all traveled the same corridors.

## Key Oasis Cities

Samarkand, Bukhara, Merv, Kashgar, Dunhuang — these were the pit stops
where caravans refueled, merchants traded, and cultures mingled. Many
remain spectacular even in ruin.

## Decline

The Silk Road faded for several reasons: the fall of the Mongol Yuan
dynasty (which had kept the routes secure), the Black Death disrupting
whole regions, and — most consequentially — the opening of maritime
routes around Africa. Once ships could carry spices directly, the land
routes couldn't compete.

## Parallels to [[roman-empire]]

Both empires' fortunes tied directly to long-distance trade. Rome's
wealth came partly from taxing goods that arrived via the Silk Road.
When Rome fell in the West, demand for eastern luxuries dropped
sharply.
