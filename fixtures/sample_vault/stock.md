# Stock

Stock is the foundation of an enormous share of Western cooking — sauces,
soups, risottos, braises. Made right, it's clear, gelatinous when
chilled, and full of savory depth. Made poorly, it's cloudy, thin, or
bitter.

## The Essentials

Bones, aromatic vegetables (mirepoix: onion, carrot, celery), and water.
Herbs and spices optional. Salt is traditionally omitted so the cook can
reduce stock later without overconcentrating salt.

## Chicken Stock

Start with roasted or raw bones. Cold water to cover. Bring slowly to a
bare simmer — never a boil; boiling emulsifies fat into the liquid and
makes it cloudy. Skim the scum that rises in the first 20 minutes.
Simmer 3–6 hours. Strain, cool, skim fat.

## Beef / Veal Stock

Roast bones first for deeper color and flavor. Add a dab of tomato paste
to the roasting pan for the last 10 minutes — it caramelizes and adds
complexity. Simmer 6–12 hours; longer pulls more collagen into
solution, which sets into gelatin on cooling.

## Vegetable Stock

Quicker — 30–45 minutes is plenty. Longer extracts bitter compounds from
vegetable skins. Avoid cruciferous vegetables (cabbage, broccoli) and
starchy ones (potatoes); use mushroom trimmings for umami depth.

## Why It Works

Gelatin from collagen gives body. Slow extraction of amino acids
(especially glutamates) provides savory depth via umami. Aromatics fill
in the top notes. Done right, stock is liquid architecture.
