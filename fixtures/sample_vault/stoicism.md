# Stoicism

Stoicism is a Hellenistic school of philosophy founded in Athens by Zeno
of Citium around 300 BC. Its most influential later practitioners were
Roman: Seneca the Younger, Epictetus, and the emperor Marcus Aurelius.

## Core Doctrine

The Stoics held that virtue — living in accordance with nature and reason
— is the only true good. External things like wealth, health, and
reputation are "preferred indifferents" — nice to have, but not necessary
for a good life.

The central practical claim: you cannot control external events, but you
can control your judgments about them. Suffering comes not from
circumstances but from our beliefs about those circumstances.

## Key Practices

- **Premeditatio malorum**: imagining the worst that could happen, to
  soften its impact if it arrives.
- **View from above**: imagining yourself from a cosmic perspective, to
  put petty concerns in scale.
- **Dichotomy of control**: at every moment, asking what is up to you
  and what is not. Focus on the former; accept the latter.

## Influence

Stoicism influenced early Christian thought, the Enlightenment, and modern
cognitive behavioral therapy. The 21st century saw a revival, partly
through popular books by Ryan Holiday and William Irvine. It cross-
pollinates interestingly with [[ethics]] and [[free-will]].
