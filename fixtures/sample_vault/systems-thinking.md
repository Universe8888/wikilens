# Systems Thinking

Systems thinking is the practice of analyzing problems by looking at the
whole — the connections, feedback loops, and emergent behaviors of a
system — rather than isolating individual parts. It originated in
cybernetics and ecology in the mid-20th century and has since spread to
engineering, management, public policy, and ecology itself.

## Key Concepts

- **Feedback loops**: outputs that circle back to affect inputs.
  Reinforcing (positive) loops amplify change; balancing (negative)
  loops resist it.
- **Stocks and flows**: quantities that accumulate (stocks) and the
  rates that change them (flows). The bathtub is the canonical example
  — water level is the stock, faucet and drain are the flows.
- **Delays**: most real systems have lag between cause and effect.
  Ignoring delays is how you overshoot.
- **Leverage points**: places in a system where a small change in one
  variable produces a large change in behavior. Donella Meadows famously
  ranked 12 leverage points, with paradigm shifts at the top.

## Unintended Consequences

Systems thinkers emphasize that complex systems often defeat well-
intentioned interventions. Introduce a predator to control pests, end
up with a worse pest problem. Subsidize a behavior you want, create
dependency. The lens doesn't guarantee better decisions, but it makes
you ask more honest questions before acting.

## Connection

Systems thinking pairs naturally with the [[scientific-method]] (for
validating causal claims) and with the broader project of good judgment
under uncertainty.
