# Thermodynamics

Thermodynamics is the physics of heat, energy, and work. It was developed
in the 19th century to understand steam engines — and ended up constraining
everything from stars to living cells.

## The Four Laws

**Zeroth law**: If two systems are each in thermal equilibrium with a
third, they are in thermal equilibrium with each other. This is what lets
us define temperature.

**First law**: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed.
A steam engine turns heat into work; a refrigerator uses work to move heat.

**Second law**: The entropy of an isolated system tends to increase over
time. Things disperse, mix, decay — time has a direction. This is arguably
the most profound law in physics. You cannot unbreak an egg.

**Third law**: As temperature approaches absolute zero, entropy approaches
a minimum (zero for a perfect crystal). Absolute zero itself is
unreachable.

## Entropy in Plain Language

Entropy is often glossed as "disorder," but it's really about the number
of microscopic arrangements consistent with a given macroscopic state. A
shuffled deck has higher entropy than a new deck because there are vastly
more shuffled configurations than ordered ones.

## Implications for Life

Living systems appear to decrease entropy locally — a cell is highly
ordered. This is only possible because cells continuously import low-
entropy energy (food, sunlight) and export high-entropy waste (heat, CO2).
The universe's entropy still increases; life is a local exception, not a
violation. See [[cellular-respiration]] for the cellular-scale story.
