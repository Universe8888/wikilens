# Writing Well

Writing well is mostly cutting. First drafts are almost always too long,
too cautious, too cluttered with hedges and throat-clearing. The craft
is subtraction.

## A Few Principles

- **Lead with the point**. Readers scan. Put the conclusion near the
  top; support it below. Newspaper journalists call this the inverted
  pyramid.
- **Short words beat long ones**. "Use" beats "utilize." "Start" beats
  "commence." Latinate words feel official; Anglo-Saxon words feel
  human.
- **Active voice usually beats passive**. "The committee approved the
  plan" is clearer than "the plan was approved by the committee." Use
  passive only when the actor genuinely doesn't matter.
- **Cut qualifiers**. "Very," "really," "quite," "somewhat" — almost
  always removable. The sentence gets stronger without them.
- **One idea per sentence**. Dense sentences that load up multiple
  clauses force readers to reparse. Give them easier bites.

## What Strunk and White Got Right, and Wrong

*The Elements of Style* was the 20th-century bible of concise writing.
Its advice on concision is mostly good; its grammar advice is often
wrong or outdated. Use the first half; ignore the second.

## The Hard Part

All of the above is the mechanical layer. The hard part — having
something worth saying — is not a writing problem. It's a thinking
problem, and thinking problems don't have style guides.
